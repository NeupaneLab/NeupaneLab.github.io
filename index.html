Plant Phylogenetics Lab

Department of Biology, Ball State University

In our lab, we construct evolutionary trees of living organisms, mainly plants, known as phylogenies. We utilize DNA and morphological trait data to build these phylogenies. By analyzing these phylogenies, we investigate the how, where, and when different groups of plants (and animals) have evolved over macroevolutionary timescales. Additionally, we contribute to organizing the vast diversity of living organisms into distinct groups based on their evolutionary relationships, a field known as systematics.

Research

My work centers around three major themes within the coffee family (Rubiaceae), particularly focusing on the tribe Spermacoceae.

1. Systematics & Phylogenetics of the Coffee Family

Rubiaceae is one of the world’s largest flowering-plant families (~13,000 species). Our lab investigates the pantropical tribe Spermacoceae (~1,000 spp., 60 genera), tackling its complex taxonomy by integrating nuclear and plastid DNA markers with detailed morphological datasets to produce a stable, global phylogeny.

2. Macroevolutionary Processes

Spermacoceae provides an exceptional framework to test macroevolutionary hypotheses. We examine:

  • Adaptive and insular radiations in Hawaiian Kadua and Pacific Hedyotis,
  • Evolution of secondary woodiness in montane clades,
  • Dynamics of heterostyly versus homostyly breeding systems,
  • Polyploidy & hybridization events (e.g., in Houstonia),
  • Speciation mechanisms across tropical mountains and islands.

3. Phylogenomics & Data Congruence

Leveraging targeted sequence capture, we generate hundreds of nuclear loci to reconstruct species trees. We then evaluate gene-tree discordance and data combinability using both empirical and simulated datasets, refining best practices for robust phylogenomic inference.
