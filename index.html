Plant Phylogenetics Lab

Department of Biology , Ball State University

In our lab, we construct evolutionary trees of living organisms, mainly plants, known as phylogenies. We utilize DNA and morphological trait data to build these phylogenies. By analyzing these phylogenies, we investigate how and when different groups of plants (and animals) have evolved over macroevolutionary timescales. Additionally, we contribute to organizing the vast diversity of living organisms into distinct groups based on their evolutionary relationships, a field known as systematics.

1. Macroevolutionary Dynamics and Speciation in Tropical Plants

The extraordinary diversity of angiosperms worldwide reflects macroevolutionary processes unfolding over deep time, generating both broad distributions and extreme endemism. These forces—most intense in tropical mountains and islands—produce novel traits, reproductive strategies, and ultimately new species. Our lab uses the pantropical tribe Spermacoceae (~1,500 spp.) as a model system to investigate how trait evolution, breeding-system shifts, and genomic change interact to generate biodiversity. We integrate field, phylogenetic, and genomic approaches to reveal how microevolutionary mechanisms scale up to macroevolutionary patterns.

Trait Lability, Polyploidy, and the Genomics of Speciation

Speciation is a multi-level process shaped by the interplay of reproductive-system evolution, hybridization, and genomic divergence across space and time. Spermacoceae exemplifies exceptional trait lability:

  • Breeding-system diversity. Distylous Houstonia caerulea vs. homostylous H. pusilla; “leaky dioecy” in island Kadua and Nesohedyotis.
  • Growth-form transitions. Independent woodiness in Hedyotis, Kadua, and Arcytophyllum linked to montane and insular colonization.
  • Morphological lability. Shifts in fruit, seed, and pollen traits blur taxonomic boundaries.
  • Polyploidy & hybridization. Autopolyploid Houstonia breaks diploid barriers (Church & Taylor 2005) and fuels gene flow among tetraploids (Glennon & Church 2015).

Hybridization can either homogenize lineages through ongoing gene flow or—via adaptive introgression and allopolyploidy—accelerate divergence and spawn novel hybrid taxa. Which outcome prevails depends on selection pressures, recombination patterns, and the genomic architecture of barrier loci.

2. Taxonomic Challenges in Spermacoceae

The high degree of morphological polymorphism and homoplasy in Spermacoceae has made generic delimitation particularly challenging. Historical confusion arose from overlapping traits dating back to Linnaeus (e.g., Diodia, Hedyotis, Houstonia). Phylogenetic studies (Kårehed et al. 2008; Groeninckx et al. 2009) reveal several genera are polyphyletic, prompting ongoing revisions with international collaborators.

3. Phylogenomic Congruence & Data Discordance

Reconstructing a reliable species tree can be challenged by gene-tree discordance from sampling error or incomplete lineage sorting. With large-scale genomic data, this discordance intensifies. We assess discordance using Bayes factors on simulated and empirical datasets to distinguish stochastic vs. biological causes before inferring species trees.

Our Current Research Questions

  • Geographic trait evolution: How do breeding-system shifts, hybridization, and polyploidy differ between island vs. continental clades?
  • Polyploid evolution in Houstonia–Stenaria: How frequent are independent polyploidization events and their impact on diversity?
  • Genomic speciation mechanisms: In hyperdiverse, endemic clades like Hedyotis and Kadua, do adaptive radiation or reticulate processes dominate? Which genomic signatures prevail—reinforcement, adaptive introgression, or hybrid speciation?
  • Evolution of woodiness: What genomic changes drive repeated herbaceous-to-woody transitions in montane Hedyotis?
  • Macroevolutionary correlates: Which traits—woodiness, breeding-system diversity, or polyploidy—best predict elevated diversification rates?

By combining fieldwork, phylogenomics, population genomics, comparative transcriptomics, and macroevolutionary modeling, we aim to elucidate how micro- and macroevolutionary processes interact to shape the spectacular diversity of tropical, insular, and continental plant lineages.
